# PITI Calculator: The Two-Fifths of a US Mortgage Payment Most Buyers Forget to Add

**Meta description:** See how property tax and homeowners insurance turn a $2,340 mortgage payment into something closer to $3,140 — and why lenders bundle both into escrow.

A reader ran the numbers on that same $412,500 home from our Mortgage Calculator article and got $2,340 a month. Then their pre-approval letter came back showing $3,140. Nobody had raised their rate. What happened is PITI — Principal, Interest, Taxes, and Insurance — the actual number lenders qualify you against, not just the P&I your calculator first spits out.

## What PITI actually stands for, and why it's bundled

In most of the US, when you take out a mortgage, your lender doesn't just collect principal and interest. They also collect a monthly slice toward your annual property tax bill and homeowners insurance premium, hold it in an escrow account, and pay those bills on your behalf when they're due. It's not optional in most cases — lenders require escrow on the majority of conventional loans with less than 20% down, and on virtually all FHA and VA loans regardless of down payment.

This is genuinely different from a lot of the world. In some countries, property tax is a separate annual bill the homeowner pays directly. In the US mortgage system, it's baked into the same monthly draft as your loan payment.

## The math

PITI = Principal + Interest + 1/12 of annual property tax + 1/12 of annual homeowners insurance premium (+ PMI if applicable, + HOA dues if applicable, though HOA usually isn't escrowed by the lender).

| Component | Monthly | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Principal & Interest | $2,340 | On $371,250 @ 6.47%, 30-yr |
| Property tax | $515 | Based on ~1.5% annual rate on $412,500 |
| Homeowners insurance | $145 | Roughly $1,740/year, mid-range coverage |
| PMI | $140 | Estimated ~0.45%/year on the loan balance, since down payment is under 20% |
| **Total PITI** | **≈ $3,140** | |

Property tax rates vary enormously by state and even by county — from under 0.3% of home value annually in Hawaii to over 2% in parts of New Jersey and Illinois. That single line item can shift your PITI by hundreds of dollars for an identical loan.

## Common mistakes

Buyers moving between states get burned by this constantly — someone relocating from a low-tax state to a high-tax one often underestimates their new payment by $300-500/month, purely on the tax line, without the mortgage terms changing at all.

Another mistake: assuming your first year's escrow estimate is locked in. Lenders re-run an escrow analysis annually, and if your insurance premium or tax bill went up, your monthly PITI goes up with it — even if your rate and P&I never change. This is why a fixed-rate mortgage can still have a payment that rises over time.

A third mistake: not shopping homeowners insurance separately. Lenders will often quote a placeholder estimate before you've picked a policy, and the real premium can come in meaningfully different once you compare carriers.

## Where PITI falls short as a full budget number

PITI doesn't include HOA dues, which many lenders factor into qualifying but don't escrow. It doesn't include maintenance — a commonly cited rule of thumb is budgeting 1-2% of home value annually for upkeep, separate from what shows up in any mortgage calculator. And it's calculated on your first year's tax and insurance estimates, which for a new construction home or a recent sale can be based on outdated assessed values that jump significantly after the county reassesses.

## FAQ

**Can I avoid escrow and pay taxes/insurance myself?**
Sometimes, if you put down 20% or more on a conventional loan — some lenders allow a waiver, occasionally for a small fee. FHA and VA loans generally require escrow regardless of down payment.

**Why did my PITI go up even though my rate is fixed?**
Almost always a tax or insurance increase, not the loan itself. Your escrow account gets reanalyzed annually.

**Does PITI include PMI?**
It's often lumped in for budgeting purposes when down payment is under 20%, though technically PMI is a separate line, not one of the four PITI letters.

**How accurate is the tax estimate before I own the home?**
It's an estimate based on the current assessed value — if you're buying at a higher price than the current assessment, expect your actual tax bill (and PITI) to rise after the county reassesses post-sale.

**Is a lower PITI always the better deal?**
Not necessarily — a lower monthly PITI from a longer term or an ARM can mean more total interest or future rate risk. Compare the full picture, not just the monthly number.
