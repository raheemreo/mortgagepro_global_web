What is PITI in a US Mortgage?

PITI stands for Principal, Interest, Taxes, and Insurance — the four components of a complete monthly mortgage payment in the United States. Lenders use your total PITI to calculate your front-end debt-to-income ratio (DTI), which should typically not exceed 28% of your gross monthly income for mortgage approval.

When is PMI Required?

Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) is required on conventional US mortgages when the down payment is less than 20%, resulting in an LTV ratio above 80%. PMI typically costs 0.5%–1.5% of the loan amount annually and can be cancelled once your LTV reaches 80% through principal repayment or home value appreciation, as required by the Homeowners Protection Act of 1998.

30-Year vs 15-Year Mortgage: Which is Better?

A 30-year mortgage offers lower monthly payments but costs significantly more in total interest. A 15-year mortgage has higher monthly payments but builds equity faster and carries a lower interest rate (currently about 0.71% lower than 30-year rates). For most buyers who plan to stay in a home long-term, the 15-year option saves substantially — often $100,000+ in interest on a $400,000 loan.

US Mortgage FAQs

  • PITI stands for Principal, Interest, Taxes, and Insurance — the four components of a complete monthly mortgage payment in the USA. Principal is the portion reducing your loan balance, interest is the lender's fee, taxes are your monthly property tax escrow, and insurance covers homeowners insurance. Lenders use your total PITI payment to calculate your front-end debt-to-income ratio.

  • Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI) is required on conventional US mortgages when the down payment is less than 20% of the purchase price, resulting in a Loan-to-Value (LTV) ratio above 80%. PMI typically costs 0.5%–1.5% of the loan amount annually. Under the Homeowners Protection Act, lenders must automatically cancel PMI when the loan balance reaches 78% of the original purchase price.

  • Most US mortgage lenders require a minimum credit score of 620 for conventional loans, 580 for FHA loans (500–579 with 10% down), and typically 640 for USDA loans. VA loans have no official minimum, but most lenders set their own floor at 580–620. Higher credit scores qualify for lower interest rates — the difference between a 620 and a 760 score can save $50,000+ in interest on a 30-year mortgage.
